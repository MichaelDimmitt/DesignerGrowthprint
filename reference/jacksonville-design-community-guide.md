# PART ONE: CAPTURING AND ORGANIZING COMMUNITY KNOWLEDGE

## Chapter 1: The Philosophy of Knowledge Capture

### Understanding What Knowledge Actually Means

Before selecting tools and platforms, you need to establish what "knowledge" means in this context. Design knowledge exists on a spectrum from explicit (documentable, teachable, transferable through writing and instruction) to tacit (intuitive, experiential, transferable only through observation and practice). Your knowledge repository strategy must address both.

**Explicit Knowledge Examples:**
- Design system documentation and rationale
- Case studies with process breakdowns
- Tool tutorials and workflow guides
- Industry research and trend analysis
- Salary and career progression data
- Company culture and interview insights
- Template libraries and component sets
- Accessibility guidelines and checklists

**Tacit Knowledge Examples:**
- How to read a room during stakeholder presentations
- When to push back on requirements versus accommodate them
- How to build credibility with engineering teams
- The intuition for knowing when a design "feels right"
- Managing difficult client relationships
- Navigating organizational politics
- Knowing which battles to fight and which to let go

The most valuable communities master the art of converting tacit knowledge into explicit artifacts while preserving the nuance that makes tacit knowledge so powerful in the first place.

### The Knowledge Lifecycle Model

Every piece of community knowledge moves through stages:

1. **Generation**: Someone has an insight, solves a problem, or develops a new approach
2. **Articulation**: That insight is expressed verbally, visually, or in writing
3. **Capture**: The articulation is recorded in a retrievable format
4. **Organization**: The captured knowledge is categorized, tagged, and made findable
5. **Distribution**: The knowledge is pushed to or pulled by those who need it
6. **Application**: Someone uses the knowledge in their work
7. **Validation**: The knowledge proves useful (or not) in practice
8. **Evolution**: The knowledge is updated, refined, or deprecated based on validation

Your infrastructure must support all eight stages. Most communities fail because they optimize for capture while neglecting articulation (making it too burdensome to contribute) or distribution (creating repositories that no one actually visits).

## Chapter 2: Tools and Platforms for Knowledge Infrastructure

### Tier 1: Communication and Real-Time Collaboration

**Slack (Recommended Primary Platform)**

Slack should serve as your community's nervous systemâ€”the place where real-time conversation happens and where knowledge begins its journey from tacit to explicit. The key is structuring your workspace intentionally rather than letting channels proliferate randomly.

*Recommended Channel Architecture:*

- **#general** - Announcements only; heavily moderated
- **#introductions** - New member welcomes and bios
- **#random** - Casual conversation, memes, off-topic banter
- **#events** - Event announcements and RSVPs
- **#jobs-board** - Job postings and career opportunities
- **#job-seekers** - For those actively looking (separate from postings)
- **#feedback-requests** - Members asking for design critiques
- **#wins** - Celebrating promotions, launches, accomplishments
- **#tools-and-resources** - Tool recommendations and tutorials
- **#industry-news** - Sharing and discussing design news
- **#learning** - Course recommendations, book clubs, study groups
- **#freelance** - Freelancer community and project opportunities
- **#leadership-corner** - For the leadership team's coordination

*Specialty Channels (Create Based on Member Interest):*

- **#ux-research** - Research methods, participant recruitment, analysis
- **#ui-visual-design** - Visual design, typography, illustration
- **#design-systems** - Component libraries, tokens, documentation
- **#product-strategy** - Product thinking, roadmapping, metrics
- **#accessibility** - A11y standards, testing, inclusive design
- **#design-ops** - Operations, processes, team scaling
- **#ai-design** - AI tools, prompt design, emerging tech
- **#motion-design** - Animation, micro-interactions, prototyping
- **#career-advice** - Mentorship, career pathing, skill development

*Slack Best Practices:*

1. Create a robust onboarding workflow using Slack's Workflow Builder that guides new members through channel subscriptions, introductions, and community norms
2. Implement a "Friday Wins" thread tradition where members share weekly accomplishments
3. Use Slack's Canvas feature to pin living documents in channels (style guides, FAQs, resource lists)
4. Schedule weekly automated prompts: "What are you working on?" or "What's one thing you learned this week?"
5. Create a custom emoji set representing Jacksonville identity (Jaguars, beaches, local landmarks)
6. Establish clear guidelines about promotional content to prevent the space from becoming spammy
7. Archive inactive channels quarterly to maintain clarity

**Discord (Alternative or Supplement)**

Discord offers similar functionality to Slack with some advantages for community-building: voice channels for spontaneous conversations, better mobile experience for casual use, more robust role and permission systems, and a more "club-like" atmosphere that can foster stronger social bonds. The downside is that Discord may feel less professional to some members, and the gaming-centric heritage can create perception issues when approaching corporate sponsors.

If you choose Discord, consider using it for the social and informal aspects of community (hangouts, gaming sessions, casual voice chats) while maintaining Slack for professional discussions and knowledge work.

### Tier 2: Knowledge Repository and Documentation

**Notion (Recommended Primary Repository)**

Notion offers the ideal balance of flexibility, accessibility, and collaboration features for a design community. Its database capabilities allow you to create sophisticated knowledge management systems while maintaining an interface that's approachable for non-technical contributors.

*Recommended Notion Architecture:*

**Home Dashboard**
- Welcome message and community mission
- Quick links to most-used resources
- Upcoming events calendar embed
- Recent additions highlight
- Member spotlight rotation

**Member Directory Database**
- Profile photo
- Name and pronouns
- Current role and company
- Years of experience
- Specializations (multi-select: UX Research, Visual Design, etc.)
- Industries worked in
- Skills offered for mentorship
- Skills seeking mentorship in
- Contact preferences
- Portfolio/LinkedIn links

**Case Study Library Database**
- Project title and thumbnail
- Contributor (relation to Member Directory)
- Company (with permission indicators)
- Industry
- Project type (redesign, 0-1, design system, etc.)
- Key challenges addressed
- Methods and tools used
- Outcomes and metrics
- Lessons learned
- Full case study content (embedded or linked)

**Resource Library Database**
- Resource title
- Type (article, book, course, tool, template)
- Topic tags
- Skill level (beginner, intermediate, advanced)
- Contributor and contribution date
- Description
- Link or embedded content
- Community rating/reviews

**Event Archive Database**
- Event title and date
- Event type (meetup, workshop, unconference, etc.)
- Speakers and facilitators
- Attendee count
- Recording link (if available)
- Slide deck link
- Key takeaways summary
- Discussion notes

**Job Board Database**
- Job title
- Company
- Location/remote status
- Experience level
- Salary range (when available)
- Job description
- Application link
- Posted date
- Referral contact (community member if applicable)

**Company Insights Wiki**
- Company profiles with design team info
- Interview processes and tips
- Culture assessments from members
- Design maturity evaluations
- Growth opportunities within each org

*Notion Best Practices:*

1. Create templates for every database to ensure consistent formatting
2. Assign "knowledge stewards" to each major section responsible for curation
3. Implement a quarterly review process to archive outdated content
4. Create a "contribution guide" page explaining how to add content
5. Use Notion's commenting system for threaded discussions on resources
6. Set up a "new additions" feed that syndicates to Slack
7. Consider Notion's Team plan for better permissions management

**Confluence (Enterprise Alternative)**

If your community skews heavily toward members at larger enterprises, Confluence may feel more familiar and professional. It's also better suited for highly structured documentation needs. The trade-off is less flexibility in design and database features, plus a steeper learning curve for casual contributors.

**GitBook (Documentation-Focused Alternative)**

For communities focused on publishing polished, searchable documentation (design system references, comprehensive guides, technical standards), GitBook offers a more refined reading experience than Notion. It's particularly strong for versioned documentation that evolves over time.

### Tier 3: Content Creation and Publishing

**Medium Publications**

Medium remains the default platform for design writing, which means publishing there maximizes discoverability by designers outside your community. Creating a "Jacksonville Design Collective" publication allows you to:

- Aggregate member contributions under a unified brand
- Build SEO presence for "Jacksonville design" and related terms
- Provide members with a credible publishing outlet
- Create a portfolio of community thought leadership

*Publication Strategy:*

1. Establish clear editorial standards and a review process
2. Create a rotating "editor of the month" role from leadership
3. Set a publication cadence (aim for 2-4 articles monthly)
4. Develop recurring series: "Designer Spotlights," "Company Culture Reviews," "Tool Deep-Dives"
5. Cross-post summaries to LinkedIn and your Slack community
6. Create a pitch process for members to propose article ideas

**Substack Newsletter**

A weekly or bi-weekly newsletter serves as the heartbeat of community communication. Unlike Slack (which is opt-in and can be overwhelming), email reaches members wherever they are and creates a consistent touchpoint.

*Newsletter Content Framework:*

- Community news and announcements
- Upcoming events with RSVP links
- Featured member or company spotlight
- Top resources shared that week
- Job opportunities
- Outside-the-community design news
- "Overheard in Slack" highlights
- Leadership reflections or letters

**YouTube Channel**

Video content dramatically extends your reach and creates persistent educational assets. Categories to consider:

- Event recordings (talks, panels, workshops)
- Tool tutorials from community experts
- "Day in the Life" at local design teams
- Portfolio review recordings (with permission)
- Interview prep and career advice
- Design process walkthroughs

Invest in basic production quality: decent audio, simple lower-thirds, consistent intro/outro. Poor production values reflect on the community's professionalism.

**Podcast**

A podcast featuring conversations with local design leaders creates intimate content that community members can consume during commutes or workouts. Formats to consider:

- Interview-based: conversations with Jacksonville designers
- Roundtable: panel discussions on hot topics
- Case study deep-dives: extended walkthroughs of significant projects
- "Office Hours": answering community questions

Podcast production is time-intensive, so only pursue this if you have committed volunteers willing to sustain it long-term.

### Tier 4: Visual and Portfolio Platforms

**Behance and Dribbble**

## Chapter 3: Getting Companies Involved

### Understanding Corporate Motivations

Companies engage with design communities for specific, self-interested reasons. Understanding and appealing to these motivations is essential for sustained involvement. The primary drivers are:

**Talent Pipeline**: Companies constantly need to hire designers. Community involvement gives them access to vetted, connected candidates and allows them to evaluate potential hires in low-stakes settings before formal recruiting.

**Employer Branding**: Design talent has options. Companies want to be perceived as design-forward, culturally attractive places to work. Community involvement signals investment in design as a discipline.

**Thought Leadership**: Companies benefit when their design leaders are recognized as experts. Speaking opportunities and content platforms amplify individual and organizational reputations.

**Competitive Intelligence**: Communities provide insight into what other organizations are doing, what tools are gaining traction, and where the industry is heading.

**Employee Development**: Community involvement offers professional development opportunities that companies don't have to fund or organize themselves.

**CSR and Community Relations**: Especially for larger companies, supporting local professional communities checks boxes for corporate citizenship.

**Networking for Leaders**: Design leaders at companies benefit personally from the professional relationships community involvement enables.

### Tiered Corporate Partnership Structure

Create a formalized partnership program with clear tiers, benefits, and expectations:

**Platinum Partners ($5,000+ annually or equivalent)**
- Logo placement on all materials and website
- Guaranteed speaking slot at annual flagship event
- Private talent pipeline sessions (first look at job seekers)
- Dedicated Slack channel for their recruiting
- Quarterly featured company profile
- Executive introduction to leadership team
- 10 complimentary event passes for employees
- Naming rights for one signature event

**Gold Partners ($2,500+ annually or equivalent)**
- Logo placement on website and select materials
- One speaking slot per year
- Job postings featured in newsletter
- 5 complimentary event passes
- Company profile in resource library
- Talent pipeline access

**Silver Partners ($1,000+ annually or equivalent)**
- Logo placement on website
- Job postings in community channels
- 3 complimentary event passes
- Mention in partner appreciation communications

**In-Kind Partners (venue, food, tools, services)**
- Recognition appropriate to contribution value
- Flexible arrangements based on specific offerings

### Strategies for Approaching Large Companies

Jacksonville's major employers with design-adjacent needs include FIS, Florida Blue, Black Knight (now ICE Mortgage Technology), Web.com/Newfold Digital, Availity, Fanatics, Baptist Health, Mayo Clinic, CSX, and the regional offices of major banks (Bank of America, Citi, JPMorgan Chase).

**Research and Preparation**

Before approaching any company, understand their design organization structure:
- Who leads design (VP of Design, Head of UX, Design Director)?
- How large is the design team?
- What products or services do their designers work on?
- What design tools and systems do they use?
- What's their design maturity level?
- Are they hiring? Growing? Contracting?

LinkedIn is invaluable here. Map out the design org chart. Look at the career trajectories of their designers. Read any public content their design team has produced.

**Identify Champions, Not Gatekeepers**

Don't lead with HR or corporate marketing. They'll often redirect to formal sponsorship processes that are slow and transactional. Instead, identify individual design leaders who would personally benefit from community involvement and approach them peer-to-peer.

The ideal champion is:
- Senior enough to have influence and budget discretion
- Personally interested in community involvement
- New enough to the company to still be proving themselves (they're looking for wins)
- Active on LinkedIn or other professional platforms (signals they care about external reputation)

**The Initial Ask: Low Commitment, High Value**

Never lead with a sponsorship request. Instead, offer something valuable that requires minimal commitment:

- "We'd love to feature [Company]'s design team in our newsletter. Would you be open to a 20-minute conversation about your design culture?"
- "We're organizing a panel on [topic relevant to their work]. Would you or someone from your team be interested in participating?"
- "We noticed [Designer Name] gave a great talk at [event]. Would they be open to reprising that for our local community?"
- "We're compiling a directory of Jacksonville design teams. Could you share some basic information about your team for inclusion?"

Once you've established a relationship and demonstrated value, you can escalate to partnership discussions.

**The Partnership Pitch**

When it's time to formally pitch partnership, focus on their motivations, not your needs:

"We've built a community of 200 active design professionals in Jacksonville, including senior practitioners from [list impressive companies]. Many of our members tell us they're evaluating new opportunities, and companies that are visible in our community tend to be top-of-mind when they make moves. Our partnership program is designed to make your design team visible and connected to this talent pool while also giving your designers professional development opportunities and your leaders speaking platforms. Here's what partnership looks like..."

**Negotiating Custom Arrangements**

Large companies often can't fit into standardized tiers. Be prepared to create custom packages that leverage their specific assets:

- A company with a nice event space might provide venue in lieu of cash
- A company with a catering vendor relationship might cover food
- A company with marketing resources might help with design or promotional support
- A company with learning and development budget might fund specific workshops

### Strategies for Approaching Smaller Companies and Agencies

Jacksonville's design agency and startup scene includes firms like ThisGen Digital, Station Four, and various boutique agencies, plus tech startups that might have one or two designers. The approach for smaller organizations differs significantly from enterprise:

**The Value Proposition Shifts**

Small companies can't afford premium sponsorships, but they have different needs:
- They need talent even more desperately (one hire is a huge percentage increase)
- They need credibility and visibility to compete with bigger names
- Individual employees have more autonomy to make sponsorship decisions
- Personal relationships matter more than formal processes

**Accessible Entry Points**

Create opportunities that don't require significant cash:
- **Spotlight Sponsorships**: Small companies sponsor a single event or newsletter issue ($100-250)
- **Venue Partnerships**: Agencies with nice offices host events in exchange for visibility
- **Coffee Sponsorships**: Companies provide coffee and snacks for meetups
- **Tool Sponsorships**: Companies with vendor relationships facilitate discounts or free access
- **Content Partnerships**: Agencies contribute case studies or tutorials in exchange for promotion

**The Portfolio Approach**

Many small agencies would love to add "community partner" to their credentials when pitching clients. Frame your partnership as something they can showcase:

"Partnering with Jacksonville's design community demonstrates your commitment to the craft and positions you as a thought leader in the local market. We'll provide you with a partnership badge, include you in our partner directory, and feature your work in our case study library. When clients ask what makes you different from other Jacksonville agencies, this is a compelling differentiator."

**Leverage Personal Networks**

In the small company world, who you know matters enormously. Have leadership team members personally reach out to agencies where they have connections. A warm introduction dramatically increases response rates.

## Chapter 4: Individual Involvement at the Leadership Level

### Defining Leadership in a Community Context

Community leadership differs fundamentally from organizational leadership. You don't have hiring/firing authority. You can't compel participation. People are volunteering their time amidst competing obligations. The only currencies you have are inspiration, organization, and reciprocity.

Effective community leaders:
- Create more value for others than they extract for themselves
- Make participation easy and rewarding
- Recognize and amplify the contributions of others
- Model the behavior they want to see
- Maintain consistent presence and follow-through
- Build systems that work without them

### Leadership Team Structure

A robust leadership structure distributes work while maintaining coherent direction:

**Core Executive Team (3-5 people)**
- **Community Lead/President**: Overall vision and external representation
- **Programs Lead**: Event planning and execution
- **Communications Lead**: Newsletter, social media, website
- **Partnerships Lead**: Corporate relationships and sponsorships
- **Operations Lead**: Logistics, finances, tools administration

**Extended Leadership Team (5-10 additional people)**
- **Membership Coordinator**: Onboarding, engagement, retention
- **Content Curator**: Knowledge repository management
- **Mentorship Coordinator**: Mentor matching and program management
- **Events Team Leads**: Specific people owning specific event types
- **Inclusion Lead**: Ensuring community is welcoming to all

**Committee Structure**
For significant initiatives (annual conference, major partnerships, new programs), form time-limited committees with clear charters and sunset dates.

### Recruiting Leaders

The pipeline from participant to leader requires intentional cultivation:

**Identification**
Look for members who:
- Show up consistently to events
- Contribute thoughtfully in Slack
- Offer to help without being asked
- Have skills the leadership team lacks
- Represent underrepresented perspectives
- Bring energy and enthusiasm

**Cultivation**
Before inviting someone to leadership, give them increasingly significant volunteer opportunities:
- Help with event setup/breakdown
- Introduce a speaker
- Moderate a discussion
- Write a newsletter segment
- Lead a workshop session
- Co-organize an event

**The Invitation**
Make leadership invitations personal and specific:

"I've noticed how engaged you've been over the past six monthsâ€”you're always the first to help at events, your Slack contributions are incredibly valuable, and members clearly respect your perspective. We're looking for someone to lead our mentorship program, and I think you'd be perfect for it. It's about 5 hours a month commitment, and you'd be joining an amazing leadership team. Would you be interested in discussing it?"

**Onboarding Leaders**
New leaders need:
- Clear role descriptions and expectations
- Introduction to the full leadership team
- Access to relevant tools and channels
- A "buddy" from existing leadership
- Understanding of decision-making processes
- Knowledge of community history and culture

### Maintaining Leadership Engagement

Leaders burn out. The primary causes are:
- Unclear expectations and scope creep
- Feeling under-appreciated
- Lack of autonomy
- Poor coordination with other leaders
- Insufficient support and resources
- Personal life changes that compete for time

**Mitigation Strategies:**

**Regular Leadership Meetings**: Monthly synchronous meetings (with good agendas and time-boxing) keep everyone aligned and connected.

**Clear Role Boundaries**: Document who owns what and revisit quarterly. Prevent territorial disputes and gaps.

**Celebration and Recognition**: Regularly and publicly acknowledge leadership contributions. Send handwritten thank-you notes. Create "leadership appreciation" moments.

**Term Limits and Rotation**: Prevent both burnout and entrenchment by having defined terms (1-2 years) with the expectation of rotation.

**Sabbaticals**: If a leader needs a break, create a graceful off-ramp with the possibility of return.

**Social Bonding**: Leadership team should enjoy each other's company. Occasional purely social gatherings strengthen the team.

## Chapter 5: Individual Involvement at the Participant Level

### Understanding Participant Motivations

People join design communities for various reasons, and your programming should serve them all:

**Career Advancement**: Learning skills, making connections, finding jobs
**Professional Development**: Staying current, deepening expertise
**Social Connection**: Meeting peers, finding friends, combating isolation
**Giving Back**: Sharing knowledge, mentoring others
**Identity and Belonging**: Feeling part of something, professional identity
**Inspiration**: Seeing others' work, getting motivated
**Validation**: Having their work recognized, feeling competent

### Onboarding New Members

First impressions matter enormously. A new member who has a confusing or unwelcoming initial experience will disengage permanently.

**Automated Onboarding Flow:**

1. Welcome email with clear next steps
2. Slack workspace invitation with channel subscription guidance
3. Notion workspace invitation with suggested starting pages
4. Introduction prompt in #introductions channel
5. Assignment of a "welcome buddy" from existing members
6. Invitation to upcoming new member orientation event
7. 30-day follow-up checking in on experience

**New Member Orientation Event:**
Monthly (or quarterly) sessions for new members that cover:
- Community history and values
- Tour of resources and platforms
- Introduction to leadership team
- Q&A session
- Structured networking activity

**Welcome Buddy Program:**
Pair each new member with an established member who:
- Sends a personal welcome message
- Answers questions during first 30 days
- Invites them to their first in-person event
- Introduces them to others

### Engagement Ladders

Members should have clear pathways from passive to active involvement:

**Level 0: Lurker**
- Joined Slack but rarely posts
- Attends events occasionally
- Consumes newsletter

**Level 1: Participant**
- Actively contributes to Slack discussions
- Attends events regularly
- Engages with community content

**Level 2: Contributor**
- Shares resources and knowledge
- Helps answer others' questions
- Submits content to case study library

**Level 3: Facilitator**
- Leads workshop sessions
- Gives talks at events
- Mentors other members

**Level 4: Organizer**
- Takes on event coordination
- Leads committees or initiatives
- Joins extended leadership team

**Level 5: Leader**
- Serves on core leadership team
- Shapes community direction
- Represents community externally

Design programming and recognition systems that encourage movement up this ladder.

### Retention Strategies

Member churn is natural, but excessive churn undermines community health. Retention tactics:

**Value Demonstration**: Regularly remind members of the value they're receiving. Newsletter sections like "This week, members shared 23 resources and helped 12 people solve problems" make value tangible.

**Personal Connection**: Members who form genuine relationships stay longer. Facilitate connection through small-group events, mentorship programs, and structured networking.

**Recognition**: Celebrate contributions publicly. Monthly "member spotlight" features, shout-outs in Slack, small gifts for significant contributions.

**Evolving Programming**: Keep things fresh. Survey members about their interests. Try new event formats. Respond to emerging topics.

**Win-Back Campaigns**: When members disengage, reach out personally. "We've missed you latelyâ€”is there anything we could do differently?"

## Chapter 6: Converting Participants to Leaders

### Identifying Leadership Potential

Not every engaged member wants to lead, and not every aspiring leader is suited for it. Look for these indicators:

**Positive Indicators:**
- Takes initiative without being asked
- Follows through on commitments reliably
- Communicates clearly and professionally
- Handles disagreement gracefully
- Shows genuine care for other members
- Brings ideas and suggestions, not just complaints
- Has time and energy to contribute
- Represents a perspective the leadership team lacks

**Caution Signs:**
- Wants the title more than the work
- Has a history of overcommitting and underdelivering
- Tends toward drama or conflict
- Seeks to use the community primarily for personal advancement
- Can't take feedback or criticism
- Inconsistent participation

### The Cultivation Process

**Step 1: Observation (1-3 months)**
Watch how potential leaders behave in community contexts. Do they show up? Do they help? Do others respond positively to them?

**Step 2: Small Asks (1-2 months)**
Request specific, time-limited help: "Could you greet newcomers at next week's event?" "Would you moderate the Slack discussion about [topic]?" "Could you write a short piece for the newsletter?"

**Step 3: Larger Responsibilities (2-4 months)**
Expand scope: "We need someone to coordinate the mentorship matching process for this quarter. Would you take that on?" "Would you co-lead the next workshop with me?"

**Step 4: Formal Invitation**
If they've demonstrated capability and sustained interest, extend a leadership invitation with clear expectations.

**Step 5: Leadership Onboarding**
Don't just add them to channels and expect them to figure it out. Provide structured onboarding to set them up for success.

### Creating Leadership Development Opportunities

Beyond learning-by-doing, offer structured development:

**Leadership Skills Workshops**: Sessions on facilitation, project management, communication, conflict resolution

**Mentorship from Senior Leaders**: Pair emerging leaders with experienced ones

**External Development**: Encourage attendance at conferences, courses, or certifications relevant to leadership

**Retrospectives and Feedback**: Regular structured reflection on what's working and what isn't

**Exposure to Other Communities**: Visit or connect with design communities in other cities to learn different approaches

# PART THREE: EVENTS THAT FOSTER COLLECTIVE KNOWLEDGE SHARING

## Chapter 10: Beyond the Single-Speaker Format

### The Problem with Traditional Presentations

Traditional "one speaker presents to a passive audience" events have their place, but they suffer from significant limitations:

- Knowledge flows in only one direction
- Audience expertise goes untapped
- Engagement is often low
- Networking happens only before/after, not during
- Introverts may feel excluded from post-event mingling
- Junior members may feel intimidated to approach senior speakers

Your leadership group's insight is exactly right: you want to activate the collective intelligence of the room, not just transmit from one expert.

### Unconference Format Events

Unconferences flip the script entirely, making attendees the content creators.

**How Unconferences Work:**

1. **Opening Circle**: All attendees gather. The facilitator explains the format and principles.

2. **Agenda Creation**: Attendees propose session topics by writing them on cards and announcing them briefly. Topics are placed on a time/room grid.

3. **The Law of Two Feet**: If you're not learning or contributing in a session, you have the responsibility to move to a different one.

4. **Sessions**: Proposed sessions happen simultaneously. The proposer doesn't need to be an expertâ€”they just need curiosity about the topic.

5. **Documentation**: Each session has a designated note-taker who captures key insights.

6. **Closing Circle**: The group reconvenes to share highlights and next steps.

**Unconference Principles:**

- Whoever comes are the right people
- Whatever happens is the only thing that could have happened
- Whenever it starts is the right time
- When it's over, it's over (sessions can end early or run over)

**Logistics for Jacksonville Design Unconference:**

- Venue: Need multiple breakout spaces (conference center, coworking space, university)
- Duration: Half-day (3-4 hours) for first attempt; full day once established
- Attendance: 40-100 people works well; more requires more space
- Materials: Poster boards, markers, sticky notes, timer
- Documentation: Shared Google Doc or Notion page for notes
- Frequency: Quarterly or annually

### Workshop and Hands-On Sessions

Workshops engage multiple learning modalities and create shared experiences.

**Design Exercise Workshops**

Structure sessions around collaborative design exercises:

- **Design Challenge Sprints**: Teams of 4-5 tackle a design problem in 60-90 minutes, then present their approach. The problem could be real (brought by a community member's company) or hypothetical.

- **Method Practice**: Teach a specific method (card sorting, journey mapping, affinity diagramming) and have groups practice with real data.

- **Tool Deep-Dives**: Hands-on sessions where everyone learns a specific tool feature or workflow together.

- **Critique Sessions**: Structured critique of member work using specific frameworks (what works, what could improve, what's one bold change).

**Workshop Facilitation Tips:**

- Have clear time-boxed activities
- Provide all necessary materials
- Mix groups intentionally (don't let cliques self-form)
- Use visual timers everyone can see
- Build in reflection and share-out time
- Capture outputs photographically

### Discussion-Based Formats

**Fishbowl Discussions**

Arrange chairs in two concentric circles: inner circle (4-5 chairs) and outer circle (remaining attendees). Only inner circle participants can speak. One inner chair remains empty; anyone from outer circle can join by sitting in the empty chair (someone from inner circle must then leave). This creates fluid, dynamic discussion with broad participation.

**World CafÃ©**

Multiple small tables (4-5 people each) discuss specific questions for timed rounds. After each round, participants rotate to new tables, cross-pollinating ideas. One "table host" stays to summarize previous discussion for newcomers. Excellent for exploring complex topics from multiple angles.

**Speed Consulting**

Pairs of participants take turns presenting a challenge and receiving advice. Strict time limits (5 minutes each) and structured rotation. Everyone both gives and receives help.

**Campfire Sessions**

Intimate gatherings (10-15 people) with minimal facilitation. A loose topic is proposed, and conversation flows naturally. Often held in the evening with beverages. Great for relationship-building and surfacing tacit knowledge.

**Lightning Talk Rounds**

Instead of one speaker, have 5-8 speakers each give 5-minute presentations. Forces focus on key insights. Creates variety and energy. Great entry point for first-time speakers.

### Show-and-Tell and Portfolio Events

**Design Show-and-Tell**

Members bring a project (in-progress or completed) and have 5-10 minutes to walk through their process. Focus on how and why, not just the final output. Audience asks questions and shares reactions.

**Portfolio Review Nights**

Structured portfolio review where multiple reviewers rotate through stations, providing feedback to members. Can be organized by experience level (junior track, senior track) or specialty (UX research portfolios, visual design portfolios).

**Work-in-Progress Critiques**

Members share designs currently in development and receive structured feedback. Requires psychological safetyâ€”establish norms that critique is about the work, not the person.

### Mentorship and Pairing Activities

**Office Hours**

Experienced designers offer blocks of time for 1:1 or small-group conversations. Can be organized by specialty (research office hours, career advice office hours, portfolio feedback office hours).

**Pair Design Sessions**

Two designers (often different experience levels) work together on a design challenge for 60-90 minutes. Learning happens in both directions.

**Mentorship Speed Dating**

Potential mentors and mentees have brief conversations to explore fit, then indicate preferences. Matches are facilitated based on mutual interest.

### Hybrid and Innovative Formats

**Design Book Club**

Read a design book together and meet to discuss. Each meeting, different members facilitate discussion of different chapters.

**Film Club**

Watch design-related documentaries (Design Disruptors, Objectified, Helvetica) together and discuss.

**Design Walks**

Walk through Jacksonville's physical spaces and discuss the design choices visible in architecture, signage, public spaces. Great for getting outside the digital bubble.

**Cross-Discipline Events**

Partner with other professional communities (developers, product managers, marketers) for events exploring intersection points.

**Industry-Specific Deep-Dives**

Events focused on design in specific contexts (healthcare UX, fintech design, e-commerce optimization) with a mix of presentations and discussion.

## Chapter 14: Governance Models

### Informal Collective

**Structure**: Loose group of committed volunteers with no formal hierarchy
**Pros**: Flexible, low overhead, decisions can be fast
**Cons**: Can be dependent on charismatic individuals, may lack accountability, hard to scale
**Best For**: Early-stage communities with tight-knit founding group

### Steering Committee

**Structure**: Defined core team with specific roles, but no formal legal structure
**Pros**: Clear accountability, distributed work, still relatively lightweight
**Cons**: No legal protection, can't receive tax-deductible donations, limited ability to contract
**Best For**: Growing communities not yet ready for formal incorporation

### Nonprofit Organization

**Structure**: Incorporated 501(c)(6) (professional association) or 501(c)(3) (charitable)
**Pros**: Legal protection, can receive grants and tax-deductible donations, formal legitimacy
**Cons**: Paperwork, compliance requirements, potentially slow decision-making
**Best For**: Mature communities with significant funding and long-term ambitions

### Chapter of National Organization

**Structure**: Affiliate of national organization like AIGA
**Pros**: Infrastructure support, brand recognition, national network, resources and programs
**Cons**: Less autonomy, must comply with national policies, membership fees
**Best For**: Communities wanting the backing of an established organization

For Jacksonville, I'd recommend starting as a Steering Committee model, with possible evolution toward nonprofit status or AIGA chapter affiliation as the community matures. AIGA Jacksonville already exists, so exploring how your group relates to that existing chapter would be valuableâ€”potential for collaboration, merger, or deliberate differentiation.

## Chapter 15: Leadership Team Effectiveness

### Running Effective Meetings

Leadership meetings should be efficient and energizing, not burdensome:

**Before the Meeting:**
- Circulate agenda in advance
- Require leads to update their status asynchronously (Slack, Notion)
- Identify what actually needs synchronous discussion

**During the Meeting:**
- Start and end on time
- Designate a facilitator (rotate)
- Focus on decisions and discussions, not status updates
- Time-box discussions
- Capture action items with owners and deadlines

**After the Meeting:**
- Circulate notes and action items
- Follow up on previous commitments

### Conflict Resolution

Conflict is inevitable. Handle it constructively:

**Depersonalize**: Focus on behaviors and decisions, not personalities

**Address Directly**: Don't let conflicts fester or go underground

**Seek Understanding**: Assume good intent and ask clarifying questions

**Find Common Ground**: Often conflicts arise from shared goals pursued differently

**Escalate if Needed**: If two leaders can't resolve, bring to full team or advisory support

### Preventing Burnout

Community leadership is emotionally demanding. Protect your team:

**Set Realistic Expectations**: Define time commitments clearly (e.g., "this role is 5 hours/month")

**Celebrate Wins**: Regularly acknowledge what's going well

**Allow Sabbaticals**: If someone needs to step back, create graceful off-ramps

**Distribute Work**: No single person should be a critical dependency

**Check In Personally**: Ask how people are doing, not just what they're doing

### Building Leadership Team Culture

The leadership team is a community within the community. Cultivate it:

**Social Time**: Not every gathering needs to be businessâ€”get dinner together occasionally

**Shared Rituals**: Start meetings with check-ins, end with appreciations

**Celebrate Milestones**: Birthdays, work anniversaries, personal achievements

**Constructive Feedback**: Create safety for honest feedback among leaders

**Shared Learning**: Read books together, attend conferences, bring back insights
